## Design Context

### Users
University students and staff at KMUTNB (King Mongkut's University of Technology North Bangkok) who have lost personal items on campus, or who have found unattended items and want to help return them. Administrators/staff who manage the system and verify claim requests. Users are typically young (18-25), tech-comfortable, and using the system during stressful moments (losing something) or altruistic moments (helping someone).

### Brand Personality
Trustworthy, Warm, Efficient — the system should feel like a reliable campus service, not a cold bureaucratic tool. It should convey hope ("your item can be found") while remaining professional.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone**: Clean, warm, and grounded. Use an earthy-warm palette with amber/terracotta accents against cream/off-white backgrounds. Think "campus bulletin board meets modern web app."
- **Theme**: Light mode primary. Warm neutrals, not cold grays.
- **Typography**: Use "Sarabun" or "IBM Plex Sans Thai" for Thai text readability, paired with a geometric sans-serif like "DM Sans" or "Sora" for English headings.
- **Anti-references**: NOT a dark hacker dashboard. NOT neon-on-black. NOT generic Material UI clone. NOT corporate SaaS.
- **References**: Notion's warmth, Linear's precision, Airbnb's trust-building details.

### Design Principles
1. **Clarity over cleverness** — Users are stressed (lost items) or busy (found items). Every interaction must be immediately obvious.
2. **Warm trust** — Use warm tones, friendly copy, and reassuring patterns. The system is a helper, not a bureaucracy.
3. **Progressive disclosure** — Show simple views first, reveal complexity only when needed (admin features, advanced filters).
4. **Status visibility** — Item states (Active → Pending Claim → Returned → Expired) must be visually distinct and always clear.
5. **Mobile-first utility** — Many users will be searching or posting from their phones on campus. Touch targets and readability matter.
